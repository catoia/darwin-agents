## FACEBOOK POST 1 — ADHD Adults Group

**Post text:**
---
Does anyone else have a "task shame spiral"?

It goes like this for me:
1. I need to do the thing
2. I open my to-do list and see the thing
3. I feel overwhelmed by the thing
4. I go do something else instead
5. I feel guilty about not doing the thing
6. The guilt makes the thing feel bigger and scarier
7. Repeat from Step 1

I realised recently that the problem wasn't me being lazy. The problem was that "the thing" on my list was too big. 

I've been using a method I call task decomposition — instead of putting the task on the list, I put only the FIRST action. The stupidly small first action.

"Write report" → "Open the document"
"Call doctor" → "Find the number and save it in my phone"
"Do taxes" → "Open the folder and find last year's return"

Once I start — even with something tiny — my brain engages and I often just... keep going. The starting is the hard part. Not the doing.

I made a simple printable worksheet for this. Just a structured page for breaking tasks down. Nothing fancy, just something that forces me to get specific about the first step.

If anyone wants it, comment below or send me a message! 💙

Has anyone else found systems that help with task initiation? Genuinely curious what works for different ADHD brains.

## FACEBOOK POST 2 — PhD/Dissertation Support Group

**Post text:**
---
Year 4 of my PhD and I genuinely didn't know if I was on track until I made a single wall-printout.

Here's what my "dissertation tracking" looked like before:
- A Notion database I stopped updating in year 2
- A Google Sheet with 11 tabs
- A paper notebook with advisor feedback (illegible, unsorted)
- My email search history (this was my main reference for what was discussed with my advisor)
- My anxiety (always present, zero actionable information)

I finally just made a simple one-page printout. Chapter statuses. Word count goal vs actual. Next 3 deadlines. A one-liner from each advisor meeting. And one box I fill in weekly: "Am I sleeping / eating / moving enough? Yes/No/No."

That last part sounds irrelevant but genuinely — my worst dissertation weeks matched my "No/No/No" weeks perfectly. Turns out your dissertation is a downstream output of your basic functioning, not the other way around.

If you're in the middle of dissertation hell, I made this into a proper printable PDF that you can stick on your wall. Just reply below or DM me and I'll share the link.

Also curious — how do YOU track your progress? I've seen people use habit trackers, Kanban boards, physical countdown charts... share your method if you have one! 🎓

## FACEBOOK POST 3 — Freelancers/Freelance Business Group

**Post text:**
---
Real talk: I was working harder than ever and making less money than I did two years ago.

Turns out I was pouring 40% of my time into a client who was paying 12% of my revenue. And I didn't know it because I'd never actually done the math.

I finally built a simple client dashboard — nothing complicated, just a printable page I update at the start of each month:

📋 Active projects (client, project, deadline, payment status)
⏱️ Hours logged per client (estimated vs actual — the gap is almost always shocking)
💰 Revenue per client YTD
📊 Simple profitability ratio: revenue ÷ hours

When I filled it in for the first time, I wanted to cry. I had one client I genuinely loved working with who was my most profitable per hour. And one I'd kept "because they were loyal" who was basically paying me 30% of my real rate when I accounted for scope creep and revision rounds.

Dropped the second one. Revenue went up. Stress went down.

I turned this dashboard into a printable PDF if anyone wants it — I use a printed version because I actually update it (vs the app version I kept meaning to open).

Drop a 💡 in the comments if you want the link! And share below — how do YOU track which clients are worth keeping?
